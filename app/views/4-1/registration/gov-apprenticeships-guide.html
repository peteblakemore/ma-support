Apprenticeships guide

Information about apprenticeships for people interested in becoming apprentices and employers who want to take on apprentices.

Overview

An apprenticeship is a paid job combining hands-on experience and practical training.

Apprentices train to become fully competent in their chosen professions.

Apprenticeships take between one and six years to complete.

Apprentices must be treated like all other employees, with a contract of employment and holiday leave.

Who can become an apprentice

Anyone aged 16 or over with the right to work in the UK can become an apprentice as long as they are not in full-time education.

Apprentices must spend at least 50% of their working hours in England for the duration of the apprenticeship.

Check out your apprenticeship options if you live in Scotland, Wales, or Northern Ireland.

Who can employ apprentices

Any employer operating in England that pays PAYE can offer apprenticeships to new or existing employees working for them, a connected company or charity.

Pay and conditions

Apprentices must be paid at least the minimum wage rate for their age if they are aged 19 or over and have completed their first year.

There is a lower minimum wage for apprentices aged:

  • under 19
  • 19 or over and in the first year of their apprenticeship

An apprentice’s pay includes:

  • their normal working hours
  • training that’s part of their apprenticeship (usually one day per week at a college, university or with a training provider)
  • sick pay
  • any benefits offered such as childcare voucher schemes
  • any support offered such as coaching and mentoring

Holidays

Apprentices must get at least 20 days paid holiday per year, plus bank holidays.

Use the holiday calculator to check holiday entitlement for apprentices.

Redundancy

Apprentices have the same employment rights as all other employees.

Employers should follow the process for making staff redundant if they have to make an apprentice redundant.

They should also get legal advice if they want to end the apprenticeship early for another reason.

Employers unsure if they can commit to a full apprenticeship but still keen to hire an apprentice can use an apprenticeship training agency. Employers who pay the apprenticeship levy cannot use funds from their apprenticeship service account to pay for these services.

Apprenticeship types

There is a wide range of apprenticeship training available across most job categories, from administration and animal care to teaching and tourism.

There are 2 different types of apprenticeship to choose from:

  • standards - each standard has been developed by a group of employers and sets out the core skills, knowledge and behaviours an apprentice will need for a specific occupation. Apprentices must complete an end-point assessment before completing their apprenticeship
  • frameworks - a series of work-related and professional qualifications. There is no end-point assessment for this type of apprenticeship. Frameworks are being phased out. There will be no new starts from [INSERT DATE] 2020

Apprenticeship levels

Apprenticeship levels range from 2 to 7. The higher the level, the more difficult the apprenticeship will be to complete.

The duration of an apprenticeship is generally more dependent on the apprentice’s abilities than its level.

Most apprentices will need to have a minimum standard of English and maths equivalent to a level 1 qualification (equivalent to GCSE grades 3 to 1 or D to G) before starting an apprenticeship. Some apprenticeships require specific qualifications.

How apprentices are trained and assessed

Training

Most of an apprentice’s training takes place on-the-job, working with colleagues who will mentor in specific knowledge and skills.

Apprentices must also spend 20% of their paid hours doing off-the-job training to achieve their apprenticeship. This training must be directly relevant to the apprenticeship framework or standard.

This training may be delivered in the workplace or through ‘day release’ with a training provider.

Employers can also directly deliver training to their own apprentices, although this is generally only done by large organisations training a large number of apprentices.

They must join the register of apprenticeship training providers as an ‘employer-provider’ to be eligible for government funding to train their apprentices.

Off-the-job training includes:

  • teaching of theory and practical training required in the apprentice’s standard or framework. For example, lectures, role playing, online learning or shadowing someone experienced in the apprentice’s profession
  • learning support and time spent doing assessments

Assessment

Assessing the apprentice’s knowledge and experience depends on the type of apprenticeship they’ve completed:

Standards

Apprentices doing standards have their skills, knowledge and behaviours evaluated at the end of their apprenticeship by taking an end-point assessment (EPA). This is known as synoptic assessment.

Assessment plans set out the specific criteria for each EPA.

Frameworks

Apprentices doing frameworks have their skills and knowledge continually evaluated by their training provider during their apprenticeship.

Individual qualifications might be graded, but the overall apprenticeship is simply ‘achieved’. This is known as formative assessment.

Who pays an apprentice’s training and assessment costs

The government will cover most of the cost of training and assessing apprentices. In some cases the government will cover all the costs.

Employers must not make apprentices pay for any aspect of their training.

How to get funding

Employers must firstly agree a price with their chosen training provider.

Each apprenticeship is allocated a maximum amount of government funding per apprentice. This is known as the ‘funding band’.

It’s recommended to keep the agreed price within the funding band because any extra costs above the allocated maximum must be funded by the employer.

You can find the funding band for each apprenticeship on GOV.UK.

Next employers need to reserve apprenticeship funding to enable the government to pay their training provider. This is done on the apprenticeship service.

Payments start as soon as the apprentice begins training.

Extra support for small employers

Employers with less than 50 employees do not have to pay anything towards training and assessment up to the maximum cost of the apprenticeship if their apprentice is aged between:

  • 16 and 18 years old
  • 19 and 24 years old and has previously been in care
  • 19 and 24 years old and has a local authority Education, Health and Care plan

£1,000 payment for taking on young apprentices

Employers will receive 2 instalments of £500 from their training provider if, at the start of the apprenticeship, the apprentice is aged between:

  • 16 and 18 years old
  • 19 and 24 years old and has previously been in care
  • 19 and 24 years old and has a local authority Education, Health and Care plan

Apprentice checklist

Before you become an apprentice you will need to check and sign your:

  • employment contract
  • commitment statement
  • apprenticeship agreement with the employer and training provider

It’s also worth checking you’re going to be paid at least the minimum wage.

Employer checklist

Before taking on an apprentice you will need to:

  • create an apprenticeship service account so you can reserve training funding, find training providers and recruit apprentices
  • sign the Education and Skills Funding Agency apprenticeship agreement
  • create and sign an employment contract with the apprentice
  • create and sign a commitment statement with the apprentice and training provider
  • create and sign an apprenticeship agreement with apprentice
  • read the latest apprenticeship funding rules for employers

Get government funding to train your apprentices

Employers need to set up an apprenticeship service account on GOV.UK in order to apply for funding to train their apprentices.

The service also enables employers to:

  • find the right apprenticeship for their needs
  • find providers that deliver their chosen apprenticeship training
  • create and upload apprenticeship job adverts on GOV.UK for free
  • review applications from candidates
  • add new apprentices and record their progress

Employers must give details of their PAYE scheme to the Education and Skills Funding Agency (ESFA) in order to get funding for training apprentices. This enables the ESFA to process applications accurately.
